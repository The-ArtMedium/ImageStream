# Visual Guide to Bokeh Styles

## Understanding Bokeh Quality

Bokeh (pronounced "boh-keh" or "boh-kay") comes from the Japanese word "boke" (ボケ) meaning "blur" or "haze." It refers to the aesthetic quality of the out-of-focus areas in a photograph.

**Good bokeh** = pleasing, smooth, creamy blur that doesn't distract  
**Bad bokeh** = harsh, busy, or distracting background blur

## Bokeh Shapes Explained

### ⭕ Circular Bokeh
```
What it looks like:
  ●  ●  ●
 ●  ●  ●  ●
●  ●  ●  ●  ●

Smooth round highlights
```

**Created by:**
- Lenses with rounded aperture blades (9-11 blades)
- Modern high-end lenses
- Wide open apertures (f/1.2, f/1.4, f/1.8)

**Examples in real lenses:**
- Canon RF 85mm f/1.2 L
- Sony FE 85mm f/1.4 GM
- Nikon Z 85mm f/1.8 S

**Best for:**
- Professional portraits
- Clean, minimal backgrounds
- When you want bokeh to "disappear" into smoothness
- Modern, polished aesthetic

**Character:** Neutral, smooth, unobtrusive

## The Sony Feathered Bokeh 🌊

### What Makes It Special

Sony's G Master (GM) lenses, particularly the 85mm f/1.4 GM and 100mm f/2.8 STF GM, create what photographers call "feathered" or "painterly" bokeh.

**Visual characteristics:**
- Ultra-smooth transitions (like watercolor paint)
- Subtle "render lines" (concentric softness)
- Highlights that glow rather than pop
- Almost liquid quality to the blur
- No hard edges anywhere in bokeh

**The Technical Magic:**

**STF (Smooth Trans Focus) Element:**
The 100mm f/2.8 STF GM has an apodization (APD) element - a special optical filter that gradually reduces light transmission from center to edge. This creates impossibly smooth bokeh transitions.

**XA (Extreme Aspherical) Elements:**
The 85mm f/1.4 GM uses XA elements manufactured to 0.01-micron precision. This near-perfect optical quality eliminates the aberrations that cause harsh bokeh.

### When to Use Sony Feathered Style

**Perfect for:**
- High-end portrait work
- Fashion photography  
- When subject separation is critical
- Clients expecting "that Sony look"
- Backlit subjects (creates ethereal glow)

**Settings in Bokeh Studio Pro:**
- Preset: "Sony GM" or "100mm STF"
- Blur Intensity: 25-35
- Focus Range: 100-140
- Edge Softness: 70-80%
- Highlight Boost: Enabled, 45-60%

**Real-world cost:**

- Sony 100mm f/2.8 STF GM: $1,498

## Bokeh in Different Photography Contexts

### 🐴 Equestrian Photography

**Common Background Problems:**
- Arena fencing (creates visual clutter)
- Parked trucks and trailers
- Other riders/horses in background
- Jumps and equipment
- Spectator areas
- Uneven dirt/grass textures

**Recommended Settings:**

**For Jump Shots:**
- Lens Preset: **85mm f/1.4 Portrait** or **Sony GM**
- Blur Intensity: 20-30 (keep some context)
- Focus Range: 150-200 (keep horse and rider sharp)
- Focus Point: Horse's eye or rider's face
- Vignette: 20-30% (draws eye to subject)
- Highlight Boost: Yes (sun flare on horse looks beautiful)

**For Dressage/Flatwork:**
- Lens Preset: **50mm f/1.8** (more natural)
- Blur Intensity: 15-25 (subtle)
- Focus Range: 200-300 (keep more horse in focus)
- Edge Softness: 60% (smooth transitions)

**Pro Tip for Shows:**
Click focus point on the rider's number or horse's bridle hardware - these high-contrast areas help define the sharp plane.

## Technical Deep Dive: What Creates Different Bokeh

### Aperture Shape
- More blades = rounder bokeh
- Straight blades = polygonal bokeh
- Curved blades = nearly circular bokeh
- Wide open = closest to actual blade shape

### Lens Design
**Spherical aberrations:**
- Under-corrected = soft, glowing bokeh (vintage character)
- Over-corrected = harder, defined bokeh (clinical)
- Well-corrected = smooth, neutral bokeh (modern)

**Coma:**
- Creates "comet tail" bokeh at frame edges
- Can be character or flaw depending on taste

### Distance Relationships
- Subject-to-background distance (more = better blur)
- Camera-to-subject distance (closer = better blur)
- Focal length (longer = better blur at same aperture)

**The math:** Bokeh quality depends on:
```
Background blur = (Focal length)² × Subject distance × Background distance
                  ÷ (Aperture × (Subject distance + Background distance))
```

*But you don't need to calculate this - just use the tool!*

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### ❌ Over-blurring
**Problem:** Background becomes unnatural mush  
**Fix:** Keep blur intensity under 30 for most work  
**Test:** If you can't tell what the background *was*, you've gone too far

### ❌ Wrong Focus Point
**Problem:** Blurring the subject's nose while eyes are soft  
**Fix:** Always click on the eyes or critical focus area  
**Remember:** Bokeh should direct attention, not confuse it

### ❌ Inconsistent Edge Transitions
**Problem:** Sharp subject with hard cutout-like edges  
**Fix:** Increase Edge Softness to 50-70%  
**Why it matters:** Natural bokeh has soft transitions

### ❌ Ignoring Lens Character
**Problem:** Using circular bokeh on vintage-style photos  
**Fix:** Match bokeh shape to image aesthetic  
**Example:** Hexagonal bokeh on film-look portraits

### ❌ Too Much Highlight Boost
**Problem:** Blown-out white spots everywhere  
**Fix:** Keep under 50% unless going for dreamy effect  
**Natural range:** 25-40% for realistic look

## Learning from Your Results

### Good Signs You've Nailed It:
- ✓ Can't immediately tell it was added in post
- ✓ Background is recognizable but not distracting
- ✓ Subject has natural edge transitions
- ✓ Highlights bloom pleasantly (if enabled)
- ✓ Overall image has depth and dimension

### Warning Signs You've Overdone It:
- ✗ Background is complete mush
- ✗ Subject has hard "cutout" edges
- ✗ Bokeh balls are too bright/glowing
- ✗ Your eye is drawn to blur instead of subject
- ✗ Looks "AI processed"

**Golden Rule:** When in doubt, use the comparison slider. If the "after" is obviously better but still looks natural, you've succeeded.

## Philosophy: Why Bokeh Matters

Bokeh isn't just about making backgrounds blurry. It's about:

- **Subject isolation** - directing the viewer's eye
- **Depth** - creating three-dimensional feel in 2D image
- **Mood** - smooth = peaceful, busy = energetic
- **Professionalism** - signaling skill and equipment
- **Storytelling** - controlling what information is available

Great bokeh whispers. Bad bokeh shouts.
